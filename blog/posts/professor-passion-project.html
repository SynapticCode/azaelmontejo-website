The Day I Collaborated on My Professor's Passion Project

January 2025 | Research, Collaboration, Mentorship

The Princeton campus, with its hushed Gothic spires and centuries of ingrained intellect, has been my world for nine weeks now. It is a place of wonder, a dream I still navigate with a quiet, almost reverent awe. But within this hallowed ground, the most profound revelations have come not from solitary study, but from the electric current of collaboration. Specifically, from the moment I was invited to step into the shared intellectual space of Professor Natalia Velez.

Professor Velez, whose brilliance I find as inspiring as it is intimidating—her own research has even delved into the very subject of competence—presented me with a choice over Zoom. Two assignments were laid before me, and I had the weekend to select my path. One was a solid, valuable project. The other was the formidable one: the abstract "widget engineering" experiment she had devised years ago at MIT, a complex, mathematically heavy concept she had wanted to evolve for years but never had the chance.

My attention, though, was caught. My life has always been deeply intertwined with people—leading teams, coaching, understanding group dynamics. The theory of organizational decision-making, of how humans collaborate under uncertainty, resonated with a deep, intuitive part of me. This wasn't just an academic exercise; it was the necessary study that would bring me the insight to truly implement science towards building real-world solutions. I chose the hard one. I chose her passion project.

The Bridge Between Worlds

My role was not simply to execute. It was to bridge a chasm. Professor Velez is a mathematical genius, a mind forged at MIT, Stanford, and Harvard. Her experiment was scientifically powerful, a dispassionate, cerebral exercise in decision-making. But as I engaged with it, I realized that for broader audiences, for those whose leadership experience stemmed from the visceral realities of building a little swim school community, the decisions needed to feel more intuitive, more human.

I voiced this, timidly at first, confessing that it felt "mathematically heavy, made for engineers...flip of a coin." Rather than dismiss my "non-traditional" perspective, she heard it. "But what if they are good at math?" she probed. "Then it is a niche experiment," I responded, "not many managers are going to be that mathematically gifted to solve those problems fast enough to truly make a reliable answer. To reach more people, it must be made simpler, more intuitive." Understanding that the goal was to make the science more accessible and human-centered, without losing its rigor, she welcomed the back-and-forth.

The Birth of EXOBOUND

This was the magic. My unique background—my Google Project Management certification, my entrepreneurial ventures where I learned to build for a target market, my years creating fitness programs as a personal trainer—became my arsenal. I wasn't just following instructions; I was collaborating on her vision, transforming the abstract into the tangible.

This was the birth of EXOBOUND. We took the elegant mathematical tradeoff and reimagined it. Instead of "widgets," players would command a starship fleet, facing asteroids and enemy raiders, making life-or-death decisions about adaptation versus coordination with imperfect communication. The underlying science remained 100% valid, but the human experience was utterly transformed.

The Alchemy of Collaboration

The profound beauty of this collaboration unfolded further in unexpected moments. I recall sitting in the lab, captivated, as a brilliant Harvard student presented a complex new formula she had created. The elegance and sheer marvel of her mathematical construction filled me with awe. Then, my professor, sitting right beside me, effortlessly began reading the formula, engaging in a rapid, fluid back-and-forth with the student.

They dissected it with the ease of picking apart a simple equation, yet their fascination with its creation was palpable. In that moment, surrounded by minds operating at such an exalted level, a powerful truth crystallized: my life's skills in connecting people, connecting disparate systems and organizations, were not just soft skills. They were the very conduits through which profound, tangible solutions could be implemented.

Trust and Empowerment

It reaffirmed my growing conviction: it's about being in the right place, with the right people, who can support me to actually make this happen. Sometimes, in life, you're left with an idea, a vision of how things could be, but no clear path on how to bring it to fruition. But the more I am around these exceptional individuals, the more I witness their intellectual mastery, the more I see what "possible" truly looks like.

It is then that I can begin connecting the invisible pieces, drawing on the wisdom of my own complex journey from isolation to collaboration. This project, this collaboration on EXOBOUND, has been more than research; it has been a profound lesson in trust, in the alchemy that occurs when different minds converge, and in the magic that happens when a mentor empowers you to bring your unique perspective to her most cherished intellectual pursuits.

It is a testament to the idea that true innovation is born not from singular genius, but from the powerful, shared act of building bridges.

Sometimes the most profound discoveries happen not in isolation, but in the space between different perspectives, different experiences, and different ways of seeing the world.
